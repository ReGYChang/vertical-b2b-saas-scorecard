# Scoring Rubrics — Vertical B2B SaaS Investment Scorecard

Each dimension is scored 1–10. Use the rubric below to determine the appropriate score
level. When the evidence falls between two levels, use the lower bound unless there is
a clear argument for the higher score.

## A. Market Structure & TAM

**Weight: 20%**

### 9–10: Exceptional Market
- Bottom-up SAM > $5B with clear path to expand TAM through adjacencies
- Vertical growing >15% annually (secular or regulatory tailwind)
- Market is fragmented with no dominant incumbent SaaS player
- Active spend migration from legacy to cloud (budget already allocated, just shifting)
- Strong regulatory mandate driving digitization (e.g., e-invoicing, ESG reporting)

### 7–8: Strong Market
- Bottom-up SAM $2-5B
- Vertical growing 8-15% annually
- Some incumbent SaaS players exist but significant whitespace remains
- Clear budget line item exists in target buyer's P&L
- Moderate regulatory or compliance driver

### 5–6: Adequate Market
- Bottom-up SAM $1-2B
- Vertical growing at or slightly above GDP (3-8%)
- Crowded with 2-3 established SaaS players; differentiation needed
- Budget exists but often discretionary (nice-to-have, not must-have)
- No strong regulatory tailwind; adoption is voluntary

### 3–4: Concerning Market
- Bottom-up SAM $500M-1B (borderline venture-scale)
- Vertical growing <3% or flat
- Dominated by 1-2 incumbents with strong lock-in
- No clear budget line item; sales require "creating the category"
- Buyer is cost-sensitive and resistant to SaaS pricing models

### 1–2: Non-Viable Market
- SAM < $500M with no credible adjacency expansion
- Vertical is shrinking or being disrupted out of existence
- Buyers have no technology budget or mandate
- Market is winner-take-all with an entrenched leader
- **TAM veto likely triggered**

## B. Vertical Moat & Defensibility

**Weight: 20%**

### 9–10: Fortress Position
- Product is the system of record for mission-critical vertical workflow
- Proprietary dataset that improves with network scale (e.g., industry benchmarks,
  compliance data, pricing intelligence) — genuine data network effects
- Regulatory certification or compliance requirement that takes 12+ months to achieve
- Multi-product integration so deep that rip-and-replace cost exceeds 12 months of fees
- Horizontal competitors would need 2+ years of vertical-specific R&D to match

### 7–8: Strong Moat
- Product embeds into daily workflow but is not the sole system of record
- Meaningful data accumulation but network effects are local not global
- Some regulatory/compliance advantage (certifications, audit trails)
- Integration with 3+ vertical-specific systems (ERP, hardware, platforms)
- Horizontal competitors could theoretically enter but haven't prioritized this vertical

### 5–6: Moderate Moat
- Product serves the vertical but uses mostly generic architecture underneath
- Data collected is useful but not proprietary or unique to this product
- No regulatory barrier; switching costs are primarily contractual (annual terms)
- Some vertical integrations but replaceable with generic alternatives
- Horizontal competitor could build comparable functionality in 6-12 months

### 3–4: Weak Moat
- Product is essentially a horizontal tool with vertical branding/positioning
- No unique data advantage; same data available to competitors
- Low switching costs — customer could migrate in <1 month
- Dependent on a single platform/marketplace for distribution (platform risk)
- Horizontal incumbents are actively entering this vertical

### 1–2: No Moat
- Pure commodity; undifferentiated from free or low-cost alternatives
- No vertical-specific IP, data, or workflow integration
- Customers regularly switch between competitors on price alone
- Easy for any competent team to replicate in weeks
- No defensibility mechanism identified

## C. PMF Signals & Demand

**Weight: 20%**

### 9–10: Unmistakable PMF
- >50% of new customers come from organic/referral channels
- Sales cycle <50% of vertical industry average
- NPS > 60; customers actively evangelize the product
- Usage metrics show daily/weekly engagement (not just login for billing)
- Waitlist or capacity constraints — demand exceeds ability to onboard
- Expansion happens without sales intervention (bottom-up pull)

### 7–8: Strong PMF Signals
- 30-50% organic inbound; paid channels are efficient, not desperate
- Sales cycle at or below vertical industry average
- NPS 40-60; low but non-zero referral rate
- Weekly active usage by core persona
- Some spontaneous expansion (seat additions, module upgrades)
- Retention curve flattens after onboarding period (no continued decay)

### 5–6: Emerging PMF
- <30% organic; mostly outbound-driven but conversion rates are improving
- Sales cycle is long but shortening quarter-over-quarter
- NPS 20-40; customers are satisfied but not passionate
- Biweekly usage; product is valuable but not yet habitual
- Expansion only happens through CSM-driven motions
- Some cohorts show good retention; others churn heavily (inconsistent)

### 3–4: Pre-PMF / Ambiguous
- Almost entirely outbound/founder-network-driven acquisition
- Sales cycle is long and unpredictable; heavy discounting to close
- Low NPS or insufficient data; limited customer feedback loops
- Monthly or less frequent usage — product is peripheral, not core
- High customization requests suggest product doesn't fit market as-is
- Retention data is too early or too noisy to draw conclusions

### 1–2: No PMF Evidence
- Cannot acquire customers without extreme effort or personal relationships
- Every deal requires significant customization (consulting, not SaaS)
- Users sign up but don't activate or quickly abandon
- Feedback is consistently "interesting but not essential"
- No repeatable sales motion; every deal is unique
- **"No identifiable buyer" veto may be triggered**

## D. SaaS Unit Economics

**Weight: 20%**

For pre-revenue or very early-stage companies, score based on the structural
conditions that would enable good unit economics (pricing power, vertical
willingness-to-pay, cost structure) rather than actual metrics.

### 9–10: Best-in-Class Economics
- NRR > 120% (strong expansion revenue from upsell/cross-sell)
- GRR > 92% (minimal contraction and churn)
- Logo retention > 92% annually
- LTV:CAC > 5:1
- CAC payback < 12 months
- Gross margin > 80%
- Rule of 40 score > 60%
- ARR growth > 80% YoY (or >$10M ARR if earlier stage)

### 7–8: Strong Economics
- NRR 105-120%
- GRR 85-92%
- Logo retention 85-92% annually
- LTV:CAC 3-5:1
- CAC payback 12-18 months
- Gross margin 70-80%
- Rule of 40 score 40-60%
- ARR growth 50-80% YoY

### 5–6: Acceptable Economics
- NRR 95-105% (flat — not shrinking but not expanding)
- GRR 80-85%
- Logo retention 78-85% annually
- LTV:CAC 2-3:1
- CAC payback 18-24 months
- Gross margin 60-70%
- Rule of 40 score 20-40%
- ARR growth 30-50% YoY

### 3–4: Below Threshold
- NRR 85-95% (net contraction — losing ground)
- GRR 75-80%
- Logo retention 70-78% annually
- LTV:CAC 1.5-2:1
- CAC payback 24-30 months
- Gross margin 50-60%
- Rule of 40 score < 20%
- ARR growth < 30% YoY

### 1–2: Broken Economics
- NRR < 85% (**veto triggered**)
- GRR < 75%
- Logo retention < 70% annually (**veto triggered**)
- LTV:CAC < 1.5:1 (spending more to acquire than lifetime return)
- CAC payback > 30 months
- Gross margin < 50% (service business masquerading as SaaS)
- Negative or near-zero growth
- **Revenue concentration veto** if single customer > 40% of ARR

## E. Team–Market Fit

**Weight: 10%**

### 9–10: Exceptional Fit
- Founder(s) spent 5+ years working in the target vertical at the buyer level
- Deep personal network of 50+ potential early customers who trust them
- Technical co-founder with relevant systems experience (not just web dev)
- Prior startup experience (successful exit or meaningful growth)
- Team has already built prototype informed by real customer pain

### 7–8: Strong Fit
- Founder has 2-5 years in or adjacent to the vertical
- Network of 20-50 potential early customers
- Solid technical capability (can build MVP without outsourcing core)
- Some startup or high-growth experience
- Has conducted extensive customer discovery (50+ interviews)

### 5–6: Adequate Fit
- Founder has studied the vertical deeply but limited direct experience
- Small network of potential customers (10-20), mostly from research
- Technical capability present but not vertical-specific
- First-time founder with relevant functional experience (PM, engineering)
- Customer discovery is decent but shallow (20-50 interviews)

### 3–4: Weak Fit
- No direct experience in the vertical; "discovered" it through research
- Few or no pre-existing relationships with target buyers
- Technical skills are generic; vertical-specific systems are unknown
- No startup or high-growth experience
- Limited customer discovery (<20 interviews)

### 1–2: No Fit
- Team has no connection to the vertical, buyer, or domain
- Cannot articulate buyer's daily workflow or pain points
- No technical capability relevant to the product
- No evidence of customer discovery or market research
- Red flags in team dynamics or commitment

## F. Capital Efficiency & Exit Path

**Weight: 10%**

### 9–10: Highly Efficient with Clear Exit
- Burn multiple < 1.5x (extremely capital efficient)
- Path to profitability within 12-18 months if needed
- 3+ strategic acquirers identified who have acquired in this vertical
- Platform expansion potential into 2+ adjacent TAM pools (wedge strategy proven)
- IPO-scale potential ($500M+ ARR trajectory)
- Public comps exist (Veeva, Procore, Toast-class outcomes)

### 7–8: Efficient with Plausible Exit
- Burn multiple 1.5-2.5x
- Path to profitability within 24 months if needed
- 1-2 strategic acquirers identified; PE interest likely
- Some platform expansion potential (identified but unproven)
- Growth trajectory suggests $100-500M ARR potential

### 5–6: Moderate Efficiency
- Burn multiple 2.5-3.5x
- Profitability possible but requires significant revenue growth
- Exit path exists but limited (niche vertical, few acquirers)
- Limited expansion potential beyond core wedge
- $50-100M ARR potential (solid PE outcome, unlikely IPO)

### 3–4: Inefficient
- Burn multiple > 3.5x
- No clear path to profitability
- Exit path is unclear; vertical may be too niche for strategic interest
- No obvious adjacency expansion
- <$50M ARR ceiling visible

### 1–2: Capital Destruction
- Burn multiple > 5x or running out of runway
- Business model fundamentally unprofitable (services + low margin)
- No identifiable acquirer or exit mechanism
- Vertical is so niche that no exit comparable exists
- Company is value-destructive at current trajectory
